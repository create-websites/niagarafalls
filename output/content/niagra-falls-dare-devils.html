Daredevils of Niagara

The wild stories of those who challenged the Falls—tales of stunts, survival, and tragedy.

October 24, 1901

Annie Edson Taylor: The First to Survive

On her 63rd birthday, schoolteacher Annie Edson Taylor sought fame and fortune by sealing herself in a custom oak and leather barrel. She was towed into the river and plunged over the Horseshoe Falls, emerging 17 minutes later, bleeding but alive. She famously warned, "No one ought ever do that again."

July 25, 1911

Bobby Leach: The First Man

Ten years after Taylor, English stuntman Bobby Leach made the plunge in a specially designed steel barrel. He survived but spent six months in the hospital recovering from two broken kneecaps and a fractured jaw. Ironically, 15 years later, he died from an infection after slipping on an orange peel in New Zealand.

July 9, 1960

Roger Woodward: The Accidental Survivor

Not all were stunters. After a boating accident, 7-year-old Roger Woodward was swept over the Horseshoe Falls wearing only a lifejacket. In a true miracle, he was spotted in the churning water below and rescued by the crew of the Maid of the Mist boat, becoming the only person known to survive an unprotected plunge.

A History of Tragedy

While a few survived, many more have perished attempting to go over the Falls. These stunts are incredibly dangerous and are now illegal, carrying heavy fines and the risk of imprisonment. The Falls are a natural wonder to be respected, not a challenge to be conquered.
